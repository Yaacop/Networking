Initially, PC0 successfully communicates with the gateway (192.168.1.1) and learns its MAC address through ARP, which is shown in the ARP table.
After the spoofing simulation, the ARP entry for the gateway changes to a different MAC address, indicating that a malicious device has poisoned the ARP table.
As a result, traffic meant for the gateway is redirected to the attacker, demonstrating how ARP spoofing can enable man-in-the-middle or denial-of-service attacks.
